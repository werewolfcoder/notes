
# Ethics

### 1. Definition

**Ethics** is a system of **moral principles**. It affects how people make decisions and lead their lives.

- It is concerned with what is "good" for individuals and society.
    
- It is often described as **moral philosophy**.
    


### 2. Origin

The term is derived from the Greek word **"ethos"**, which means custom, habit, character, or disposition.

### 3. What Does Ethics Cover?

According to your notes, ethics deals with the following fundamental questions and dilemmas:

- **How to live a good life**.
    
- **Our rights and responsibilities**.
    
- **The language of right and wrong** (moral decisions).
    

### 4. Types of Ethics

Your syllabus distinguishes between two main types:

- **Personal Ethics:** These are the moral values that form the character and conduct of a specific person (e.g., personal integrity).
    
- **Organization/Business Ethics:** This deals with moral issues and dilemmas that organizations face. It defines what constitutes right and wrong in a business context (e.g., fair labor practices).
    

### 5. Ethics vs. Values (Important Exam Distinction)

You will often be asked to differentiate ethics from values.

- **Ethics:** Refers to guidelines or rules for **conduct** (behavior). It tells us what is morally correct or incorrect in a given situation. It acts as a **constraint**.
    
- **Values:** Refers to principles or ideals that help a person judge what is **important**. It acts as a **motivator**





	
# Kohlbergs model

Developed by Lawrence Kohlberg, this theory suggests that people progress through stages of moral reasoning just as they progress in physical or cognitive development. He used the "Heinz Dilemma" (should a man steal a drug to save his dying wife?) to determine which stage a person is in.

![[Pasted image 20251122092442.png]]


- **Level 1: Pre-conventional Morality** (Focus: Self-Interest)
    
    - **Stage 1 (Punishment & Obedience):** Right is defined by obedience to authority to avoid punishment. Rules are fixed and absolute.
        
    - **Stage 2 (Individualism & Exchange):** Right is what serves one's own needs. "What's in it for me?" Relationships are based on fair exchange.
        
- **Level 2: Conventional Morality** (Focus: Society/Rules)
    
    - **Stage 3 (Interpersonal Relationships):** "Good boy/Good girl" orientation. Focus on being nice, winning approval, and maintaining good relationships.
        
    - **Stage 4 (Social System/Law & Order):** Focus on duty, respecting authority, and maintaining the social order. Rules must be followed to prevent chaos.
        
- **Level 3: Post-conventional Morality** (Focus: Principles)
    
    - **Stage 5 (Social Contract):** Rules are seen as social contracts that should benefit the majority. If laws are unjust, they can be challenged.
        
    - **Stage 6 (Universal Ethical Principles):** Morality is based on abstract, universal principles (like justice, equality) that apply to everyone. One follows these principles even if they conflict with laws.

# Ancient education system

the Ancient Indian Education System serves as a model to correct the flaws in the modern system, such as the lack of confidence between teachers and students and the lack of humanitarian attitudes .

Here are the key lessons and methodologies from that system:

### 1. The Core Objective: "Man Making"

Unlike modern education which often focuses on information accumulation, the ancient aim was **"training for completeness of life"** and the **"molding of character"**.

- As Swami Vivekananda quoted, education was for "Man Making and Character Building".
    
- It prepared students for the battle of life, not just for a job.
    

### 2. The Three Phases of Learning

This is the most critical technical aspect of the ancient system. Learning was not passive; it involved three distinct steps:

- **1. Sravana (Listening):** This means to listen and understand. It emphasizes that "listening" is different from merely "hearing." The student absorbs the knowledge from the Guru.
    
- **2. Manana (Reflecting):** This involves reflecting on what was listened to. It is the discussion of the truth of opinions. The Guru raises questions, and the group discusses them to understand the logic .
    
- **3. Nididhyasana (Realization):** This is the complete comprehension of the truth. The goal is for the student to **live the truth**, not merely explain it with words. It is the realization phase .
    

### 3. Brahmacharya (Discipline)

- The student life began with the _Upanayana_ ceremony and the vow of _Brahmacharya_ .
    
- This lesson teaches **self-discipline** and **self-control**.
    
- The student was expected to avoid all sorts of pleasures and strictly follow the work given by the Guru .
    

### 4. The Guru-Shisya Parampara (Teacher-Student Bond)

- The system created a strong bond between the Teacher (_Guru_) and Student (_Shisya_), which is often missing today.
    
- Through this close relationship, students learned ethics, moral values, and empathy by understanding others' lives, including the poor.
    
- **Lesson for Managers:** This "mentorship" model helps individuals think ethically before taking decisions that affect society.
    

### 5. The Role of Vedas

The curriculum was rooted in the four Vedas, which taught the meaning of life and culture:

- **Rigveda:** Teaches stages of life (family, renunciation).
    
- **Yajurveda:** Teaches performance of sacrifices/duty.
    
- **Samaveda:** Study of music.
    
- **Atharvaveda:** Study of medical sciences.




# Law of karma

### 1. Origin and Meaning

- **Definition:** The word 'Karman' is Sanskrit for **action**.
    
- **History:** It is an original Vedic thought dating back to 1500 BCE, which was later popularized by the teachings of Buddha and Mahavira around 500 BCE.
    
- **Scope:** Karma is not just physical action. It consists of our **thoughts, words, actions**, and even the actions that others perform under our instructions.
    

### 2. The Core Philosophy

The law explains the relationship between cause and effect in human life.

- **Cycle of Rebirth:** The _Shatapatha Brahmana_ explains that while bodies die, the soul is eternal and continues its journey through many lifetimes.
    
- **Self-Creation:** The soul creates a system of actions and reactions. The totality of a person's actions in this life and previous lives determines their future. Essentially, **"a man is born to the world he has made"** .
    

### 3. The Chain of Destiny

The _Brihadaranyaka Upanishad_ sums up the law beautifully with this chain of causality:

1. **Desire:** You are what your deep, driving desire is.
    
2. **Will:** As your desire is, so is your will.
    
3. **Deed (Karma):** As your will is, so is your deed.
    
4. **Destiny:** As your deed is, so is your destiny .
    

### 4. Internal Grace vs. Disgrace (Osho’s Interpretation)

Your notes provide a practical interpretation by Osho, distinguishing it from just "fate."

- **Internal State:** Karma explains what happens _inside_ our being. Every living moment, we create actions that cause either a **grace** or a **disgrace** to rise within us .
    
- **Spontaneous Reaction:** This feeling is a spontaneous reaction to our actions. Negative actions lead to suffering, while positive actions beget happiness and joy .
    

### 5. Inevitability

The law emphasizes accountability. Nobody can avoid or cheat Karma because it is impossible; once a person realizes this inevitability, they transform into a different person .





# Quality of working life 

### 1. Definition and Core Concept

- **What is it?** Quality of Working Life represents the quality of the relationship between employees and their total working environment.
    
- **Human Dimension:** It focuses on the human dimensions of work, relating directly to job satisfaction and organizational development.
    
- **The Balance:** It is defined as the balance between an employee's work demands and their outside interests or pressures.
    

### 2. Why is it Important?

Dissatisfaction with working life (frustration, boredom, anger) can be costly to both individuals and organizations.

- **Link to Profit:** The profitability of a company is directly linked to the satisfaction of its workforce.
    
- **Consequences of Poor QWL:** Ignoring it leads to increased turnover, declining productivity, and an inability to retain qualified staff.
    
- **Benefits of Good QWL:**
    
    1. Positive employee attitudes toward the company.
        
    2. Increased productivity and intrinsic motivation.
        
    3. Enhanced organizational effectiveness and competitive advantage.
        

### 3. The Three Major Components

QWL involves three specific parts that must be managed :

1. **Occupational Health Care:**
    
    - A safe work environment is the basis for enjoying work.
        
    - The work must not pose health hazards to employees.
        
2. **Suitable Working Time:**
    
    - Companies must observe standard working hours.
        
    - There must be limits on overtime and provisions for vacations and holidays.
        
3. **Appropriate Salary:**
    
    - The salary must be agreed upon and appropriate.
        
    - It should never be less than the government-mandated minimum wage.
        

### 4. The Dual Role of Work

The concept recognizes that work serves two purposes for an individual:

1. **Earning a Living:** It provides for the family.
    
2. **Self-Realization:** It provides enjoyment and satisfaction





# AUROBINDO GHOSH

Sri Aurobindo Ghosh's approach to ethics is distinct from traditional Western or even conventional religious views. He does not view ethics merely as a set of social rules ("do's and don'ts") or mental codes of conduct. Instead, he views ethics as a **means of spiritual evolution**.

For Aurobindo, the ultimate goal of human life is not just to be "good" by societal standards, but to evolve into a higher state of consciousness—to transition from a mental being to a **Supramental (Divine) being**.

Here is a detailed explanation of his ethics:

### 1. The Three Stages of Life

To understand Aurobindo's ethics, one must understand his distinction between three types of life. He argues that "morality" belongs to the ordinary life, whereas "spirituality" transcends it.

- **Ordinary Life (The Moral Stage):** This is the life of the average human, governed by the mind and social rules. Here, morality is a "mental construction" used to control our animal instincts (anger, greed, desire). It is necessary for social order but is limited because it is based on rigid rules rather than inner truth.
    
- **Religious Life:** This is a movement away from ordinary life towards the Divine, but it is often trapped in dogmas, creeds, and rituals without true knowledge.
    
- **Spiritual Life (The Goal):** This is the highest stage. It is not about following rules but about a **change of consciousness**. In this stage, one acts not because a rule says "do not kill," but because one feels the Oneness of all existence. True ethics, for Aurobindo, is acting from this spiritual truth.
    

### 2. Ethics as Evolution of Consciousness

Aurobindo famously stated that "Man is a transitional being". We are not the final product of evolution. Just as nature evolved from matter to life (plants) and life to mind (humans), it is now evolving from mind to **Supermind** (Superman).

- **Role of Ethics:** Ethics is the tool that helps us prepare for this next evolutionary leap. It purifies the mind and vital nature (emotions/desires) so that the higher Divine consciousness can descend into us.
    
- **The Psychic Being:** Unlike Western ethics which often rely on "Reason," Aurobindo teaches that our true moral guide is the **Psychic Being** (the evolving soul deep within). As this inner soul awakens, it naturally guides us toward truth, compassion, and justice without needing external rulebooks.
    

### 3. Integral Yoga and "The Life Divine"

His ethical philosophy is practically applied through **Integral Yoga**. Unlike ascetic paths that reject the world (e.g., monks retreating to caves), Aurobindo’s ethics is about **transforming the world**.

- **Involution and Evolution:** The Divine (Spirit) has hidden itself in Matter (Involution). Our purpose is to reveal that Divine Spirit through our actions (Evolution).
    
- **Transformation, not Negation:** Ethics shouldn't just suppress bad behavior (negation); it should transform our lower nature (anger, lust, ego) into their higher divine forms (strength, love, service).
    
- **Life Divine:** The goal is to live a divine life _on earth_. This means bringing peace, unity, and bliss into our daily work, politics, and society, not just finding peace in heaven after death.
    

### 4. Key Ethical Principles

|**Concept**|**Explanation**|
|---|---|
|**Transvaluation of Values**|We must move beyond customary morality (social rules) to spiritual values. True values come from realizing the Self, which requires self-sacrifice and dedication.|
|**Oneness (Unity)**|The foundation of his ethics is the realization that **All is Brahman** (God). If you hurt another, you hurt yourself. True ethics is the practice of this spiritual unity.|
|**Action (Karma Yoga)**|Influenced by the Gita, he believed in action without attachment to the result (Nishkama Karma). Work should be done as an offering to the Divine, which turns work into worship.|
|**Beyond Good and Evil**|At the highest spiritual level, the "Superman" transcends the mental duality of "good vs. bad" and acts purely as an instrument of the Divine Will.|




# Rabindranath Tagore

Rabindranath Tagore's ethics are deeply rooted in **Spiritual Humanism**, where the divine is realized through humanity. His ethical philosophy spans spiritual, political, social, and educational realms, emphasizing freedom, joy, and the holistic development of the individual.

Here is a detailed explanation of Tagore's ethics:

### 1. Spiritual Humanism (The Core Philosophy)

Tagore’s central philosophy revolves around the "Man of God"11. He believed that the Supreme Reality is essentially human and can only be realized through the love of fellow human beings2.

- **God in Humanity:** He translated the "Love of God" into the "Love of Human"3. He believed that truth cannot exist independently of humanity. In a famous conversation with Einstein, Tagore stated, "if there is any truth absolutely unrelated to humanity then for us it is absolutely non-existing"44.
    
- **Unity:** He sought the origin of spiritual aspirations in the spirit of unity expressed by early humans5. Thus, truth is realized only within human society6.
    

### 2. Political Ethics

Tagore’s political ethics focused on the rights and strength of the individual against the state.

- **Rights & Strength:** He believed that while every individual and nation must have rights, they must also possess the **inner strength** to realize and retain those rights. Without strength, rights cannot be retained even if granted by rulers7.
    
- **State vs. Individual:** He argued that the State exists for the individual, not the other way around. Its activities should aim to provide maximum freedom for the individual to attain liberty88.
    
- **Decentralization:** He opposed the impersonal nature of British rule and advocated for the **decentralization of authority**. He believed true freedom comes from empowering local self-government institutions9.
    

### 3. Social Ethics

Tagore viewed social life as the foundation of political life. He believed they could not be separated, as political life is merely a specialized aspect of social life10.

- **Social Degeneration:** He observed that Indian society had degenerated because social rulers failed to preserve social institutions11.
    
- **Synthesis of Cultures:** He highlighted that India historically represented a **synthesis of various philosophies**. He believed in reviving ancient Indian culture and that constructive efforts should be made to restore this broad-based cultural harmony12.
    
- **Solidarity:** He had deep faith in social solidarity and the inherent value of ancient Indian civilization13.
    

### 4. Educational Ethics (The Shanti Niketan Model)

Tagore was an educational revolutionary. He believed the current system was inadequate and proposed a system suited specifically to India14.

- **East Meets West:** He envisioned an education system where the cultures of the East and the West could unite and understand each other15.
    
- **Holistic Personality:** The object of education is not just to accumulate knowledge but to develop an **all-round human personality**. This includes harmonizing physical, intellectual, aesthetic, and spiritual growth into one integral process16161616.
    
- **Freedom and Joy:** He emphasized that **joy and freedom** are basic requirements for education. His system (Shanti Niketan) eliminated physical punishment, examinations, and fear, which he viewed as humiliating restrictions17.

# Vivekanand

Swami Vivekananda’s ethics are centered on **practical spirituality**. He believed that ethics are not just social rules, but a path to realizing the divine within oneself and others. His philosophy transforms "work" into "worship" and "morality" into "spiritual strength."

Here is a detailed explanation of his ethics:

### 1. The Core Philosophy: "Be and Make"

Vivekananda’s ethics can be summarized in his famous maxim: **"Be and Make."**

- **Be:** Become a person of strong character and realization.
    
- Make: Help others to become the same.
    
    He believed that the goal of all ethics and religion is "Man-Making"—molding a human being who is physically strong, intellectually sharp, and spiritually enlightened.
    

### 2. Ethics vs. Law (The limitation of Law)

Vivekananda famously argued that a nation is not made great by its parliament or laws, but by the character of its people.

- **Law is External:** Laws can force a person to act in a certain way out of fear of punishment, but they cannot change the heart. Laws are "bondage."
    
- **Ethics is Internal:** True morality comes from within. It is the manifestation of inner perfection. He stated, _"No nation is great or good because parliament enacts this or that, but because its men are great and good."_
    
- **Conclusion:** We need fewer laws and more character. If people are good, the system will be good. If people are corrupt, no amount of laws can save the system.
    

### 3. Service as Worship (Seva)

This is the most unique aspect of his ethics. He rejected the traditional distinction between "sacred" (prayer) and "secular" (work).

- **Jiva is Shiva:** He taught that every living being (_Jiva_) is a temple of God (_Shiva_). Therefore, serving people is serving God.
    
- **Daridra Narayana:** He coined the term _Daridra Narayana_ (God in the form of the poor). He argued that feeding a hungry man is more spiritual than offering flowers in a temple while ignoring the poor.
    
- **Work is Worship:** You do not need to run away to a forest to be ethical. Doing your duty with dedication and without selfishness is the highest form of worship (Karma Yoga).
    

### 4. Man-Making Education

For Vivekananda, ethics is the fruit of true education.

- **Definition:** He defined education as _"The manifestation of the perfection already in man."_
    
- **Character Building:** Information gathering is not education. True education must give you the mental strength to stand on your own feet and the moral strength to do what is right.
    
- **Strength is Virtue:** He equated morality with strength and immorality with weakness. Anything that makes you weak—physically, intellectually, or spiritually—should be rejected as poison.
    

### 5. Universal Tolerance

His ethics were not limited to one religion. He preached **Universal Acceptance**.

- He believed that all religions are different paths to the same goal.
    
- Ethical conduct involves respecting all faiths and seeing the unity in diversity.

# Gandhi


Mahatma Gandhi’s ethics are practical and action-oriented. He did not view ethics as a set of rules to be discussed in a classroom, but as a way of life to be lived every moment. His philosophy is often summarized as **"My Life is My Message."**

For your exam, you should focus on these four main pillars of his ethical system:

### 1. The Twin Principles: Truth (Satya) & Non-Violence (Ahimsa)

Gandhi believed these two were the two sides of the same coin.

- **Truth (Satya):** For Gandhi, "Truth is God." It is not just about speaking the truth, but living it. He believed that if you are true to yourself and your conscience, you are being ethical.
    
- **Non-Violence (Ahimsa):** This is the means to reach the Truth.
    
    - It is **not** just the absence of physical violence (not hitting/killing).
        
    - It includes **mental non-violence**: Not hurting anyone with your words, thoughts, or anger.
        
    - He taught that violence comes from fear and weakness, while non-violence requires immense inner strength and bravery.
        

### 2. The Doctrine of Trusteeship (Economic Ethics)

This is a very frequently asked topic in exams regarding business ethics.

- **The Concept:** Gandhi observed that there is enough in this world for everyone's _need_, but not for everyone's _greed_.
    
- **Trustees not Owners:** He argued that wealthy people (businessmen, industrialists) should not consider themselves absolute "owners" of their wealth. Instead, they should act as **Trustees**.
    
- **Duty:** A trustee is someone who manages money for the benefit of others. So, a businessman should keep what he needs for a reasonably comfortable life and use the rest of the surplus wealth for the welfare of society (the poor, education, health).
    

### 3. The Seven Social Sins

Gandhi identified seven behaviors that destroy the moral fabric of society. These are often asked as a list in exams:

1. **Politics without Principle:** Seeking power at any cost without ethical values.
    
2. **Wealth without Work:** Making money through gambling, cheating, or without effort (speculation).
    
3. **Commerce without Morality:** Doing business purely for profit while cheating customers or exploiting workers.
    
4. **Knowledge without Character:** Being highly educated but using that intelligence to harm others (e.g., a brilliant hacker or corrupt doctor).
    
5. **Pleasure without Conscience:** Pursuing self-gratification (lust, addiction) without caring about the consequences for others.
    
6. **Science without Humanity:** Technological advancements that harm mankind (e.g., weapons of mass destruction).
    
7. **Worship without Sacrifice:** Praying to God but refusing to help those in need or give up one's ego.
    

### 4. Non-Stealing (Asteya) & Non-Possession (Aparigraha)

These are personal ethical codes that apply to professional life as well.

- **Non-Stealing (Asteya):**
    
    - Standard Definition: Not taking what belongs to others.
        
    - **Gandhi’s Deeper Definition:** Taking _more than you need_ is also stealing. If you hoard resources that someone else desperately needs, you are stealing from them.
        
- **Non-Possession (Aparigraha):**
    
    - This means not being attached to material things. It encourages a simple life so that one is not tempted to be corrupt or greedy to maintain a luxurious lifestyle.

# Morality etiquets and professional codes

### 1. Morality

- **Definition:** Morality is the moral code of an individual or a society. It refers to standards of behavior that dictate what is right or wrong in a broader sense.
    
- **Nature:** It is often derived from philosophy, religion, or culture, but it is **not necessarily based on religion**.
    
- **Independence:** Moral beliefs must be justified by reason. A person holds a moral belief only after a conscientious effort to think rationally and impartially about it.
    
- **Contrast with Law:** Morality differs from law (statutes and regulations). While laws are enforced by penalties, morality is often enforced by conscience or social pressure.
    

### 2. Etiquette

- **Definition:** Etiquette is a set of rules for **well-mannered behavior**.
    
- **Nature:** It consists of unwritten codes or rules of social or professional behavior.
    
- **Example:** "Medical etiquette" refers to the polite and proper behavior expected between doctors, or between a doctor and a patient, which is distinct from the _moral_ duty to save a life.
    
- **Key Difference:** Violating etiquette might make you look rude or unprofessional, but violating morality makes you "wrong" or "bad" in an ethical sense.
    

### 3. Professional Codes

- **Definition:** These are **special rules governing the members of a profession**.
    
- **Scope:** They apply only to specific groups, such as doctors, lawyers, or engineers, rather than to society as a whole.
    
- **Function:**
    
    - Professionals earn a living by undertaking a common activity and regulating it themselves.
        
    - They form a constitution (e.g., an engineering association) and publish a **code of conduct** .
        
- **Relation to Law:** These codes must be in line with the law but are often **more restrictive** than the law. For example, a professional code might forbid a conflict of interest that is technically legal but professionally unethical.




# Indian ethical traditions

### 1. The Foundation: Dharma (Duty & Righteousness)

The central pillar of Indian ethics is **Dharma**. It is a complex term that means "that which holds together." It ensures social stability and individual growth.

- **Concept:** It is not just "religion" but a code of conduct for living. It includes duties, rights, laws, and virtues.
    
- **Two Types of Dharma:**
    
    - **Sadharana Dharma (Universal Duties):** Virtues that everyone must follow, regardless of their job or caste. Examples: _Ahimsa_ (Non-violence), _Satya_ (Truth), _Asteya_ (Non-stealing), _Saucha_ (Purity).
        
    - **Svadharma (Individual Duty):** The specific duty of an individual based on their role, profession, or stage in life. For example, a soldier’s dharma is to fight, while a teacher’s dharma is to educate.
        
- **Relevance:** In a professional context, this teaches that one must be true to their profession. A manager's _Svadharma_ is to ensure the welfare of their team and company.
    

### 2. The Law of Karma (Cause and Effect)

Indian tradition emphasizes that every action has a consequence.

- **The Principle:** "As you sow, so shall you reap." Actions (_Karma_) create an energy that returns to the doer.
    
- **Nishkama Karma (Detached Action):** This is a key lesson from the **Bhagavad Gita**. It teaches that one should perform their duty with total dedication but **without attachment to the results** (fruits) of the action.
    
    - _Why?_ Obsessing over results (profit/promotion) breeds anxiety and corruption. Focusing on the work itself brings excellence and peace of mind.
        

### 3. The Four Purusharthas (Goals of Life)

Indian tradition recognizes that humans have different needs. It provides a framework to balance them:

1. **Dharma (Righteousness):** Moral values and duties (The foundation).
    
2. **Artha (Wealth):** Economic security and prosperity. It is valid to earn money, but it must be done through _Dharma_ (ethical means).
    
3. **Kama (Desire):** Emotional and sensual fulfillment.
    
4. **Moksha (Liberation):** The ultimate spiritual goal, freedom from the cycle of birth and death.
    

- **Key Lesson:** Wealth (_Artha_) and Desire (_Kama_) are good, but they must always be regulated by _Dharma_. Earning money (_Artha_) without _Dharma_ leads to corruption (Greed).
    

### 4. Values from Scriptures (Upanishads & Gita)

- **Unity of Existence:** The philosophy that **"All is One"** (Advaita). If you hurt others, you are hurting yourself because the same divine spark (_Atman_) is in everyone. This is the metaphysical basis for ethics.
    
- **Yajna (Sacrifice):** Life is viewed as a sacrifice/offering to the divine. Work is not just for salary; it is an offering to society and the greater good.
    
- **Rta (Cosmic Order):** The belief that there is a natural moral order in the universe. Violating it creates chaos/suffering.


### 5. Trusteeship (Gandhian View)

Though modern, this concept is deeply rooted in Indian tradition (Ishavasya Upanishad).

- **Concept:** Wealth belongs to society. A businessman is not the absolute "owner" of his wealth but a **"Trustee."**
    
- **Duty:** He is entitled to a reasonable living, but the surplus wealth should be used for the welfare of the poor and society. This transforms capitalism into a tool for social good.

# Professional ethics

Professional ethics are a specific set of rules and norms that govern the behavior of members within a specific profession (e.g., doctors, engineers, lawyers).

- **Self-Regulation:** Professionals are a group of people who earn a living by a common activity and regulate this activity themselves.
    
- **The Code:** To be a profession, the group must form a constitution and publish a **code of conduct**.
    
- **Relation to Law:** This code must be in line with the law, but it is often **more restrictive** than the law.
    

### The 4 Universal Principles of Professional Ethics

Most professions share common fundamental principles which boil down to these four:

#### 1. Respect for People's Dignity and Rights

Professionals must respect the intrinsic value of the people they serve.

- **Privacy:** Respect the client's personal integrity, including privacy and confidentiality.
    
- **Non-Judgmental:** Be non-judgmental of the client irrespective of their age, behavior, culture, gender, race, or religion.
    
- **Respect for Colleagues:** Respect the knowledge, skills, and experience of colleagues and other professionals.
    
- **Competence:** If you are not competent to undertake a specific task, you must refer it to another professional.
    

#### 2. Responsible Practice

The critical focus here is limiting your practice to your specific field of expertise.

- **Knowledge & Skill:** You must possess the appropriate knowledge and skill before undertaking an activity.
    
- **Training:** You must undergo relevant training and adhere to best practices.
    
- **Continuous Learning:** You must keep abreast of (stay updated with) new developments in your field.
    
- **Supervision:** If using a new technique, you should do so under the supervision of a competent and experienced professional.
    

#### 3. Integrity in Relationships

There is often an unbalanced power relationship between the professional (who has the knowledge) and the client (who is vulnerable).

- **Trust:** For professionals to be accepted and successful, they need to be trusted by society.
    
- **Fiduciary Relationship:** This is a relationship of good faith. The client reposes confidence and trust in the professional.
    
- **Loyalty:** Good conscience requires the professional to act at all times for the **sole benefit and interests of the client**, with loyalty to those interests.
    

#### 4. Responsibility

While the client is the first responsibility, professionals also have a broader responsibility to society.

- **Whistle-blowing:** Protect public trust by reporting ("blowing the whistle" on) non-professional conduct by others.
    
- **Public Safety:** Protect society from dangerous practices.
    
- **Advancing Knowledge:** Disperse information that can advance the profession.
    
- **Social Service:** Assist in instances where worthy causes cannot afford professional services (pro bono work)





# Evolution of ethics


### 1. Early Roots: Bartering and Philosophy

- **Bartering:** The notion of ethics in business can be traced back to the earliest forms of bartering, which were based on the principle of **"equal exchange"**.
    
- **Philosophers:** Countless philosophers and economists examined this topic over centuries, ranging from **Aristotle** (concept of justice) to **Karl Marx** (critique of capitalism).
    

### 2. The Modern Era: The 1970s (USA)

- **The Turning Point:** The modern concept of "business ethics" originated in the **United States** and became widespread in the **1970s**.
    
- **The Trigger:** It emerged largely due to the rise of **anti-big business protest groups** during this decade.
    

### 3. The "Three Strands" of Development

The history of business ethics in the U.S. is viewed as the intersection of three intertwined strands:

- **Strand 1: The Ethics-in-Business Tradition:** This is the long tradition of applying ethical norms to business, similar to how they are applied to other areas of social life. It encompasses both secular and religious branches.
    
- **Strand 2: The Academic Field:** This marks the development of business ethics as a formal subject of study. It includes:
    
    - _Philosophical branch:_ Normative and critical.
        
    - _Social-scientific branch:_ Descriptive and empirical.
        
- **Strand 3: Corporate Adoption:** This represents the integration of ethics into actual business practices. It includes the commitment to **Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)**.
    

### 4. Global Expansion: The 1980s and Beyond

- **Europe & Japan:** Business ethics was introduced here in the **1980s**. However, the development varied from the U.S. model due to socio-political and economic differences.
    
- **The UN Global Compact:** On the global stage, business ethics became associated with the UN Global Compact. This was initiated by UN Secretary-General Kofi Annan in **1999** and officially launched in **July 2000**.
    

### 5. Present Day Status

- **Internalization:** Today, companies realize they need to manage and internalize ethics.
    
- **Legislation & CSR:** Thanks to government legislation, ethics are now reflected in corporate strategies and **codes of conduct**.
    
- **Academic Status:** It is now a firmly established academic field with both philosophical and empirical branches.

# Root causes of unethical behaviour 


### 1. No Code of Ethics

- **Problem:** Employees are more likely to do wrong if they simply do not know what is right. Without clear guidelines, they may act unscrupulously.
    
- **Solution:** A code of ethics acts as a proactive tool to establish the organization's values and set clear boundaries for everyone.
    

### 2. Fear of Reprisal

- **Problem:** People often witness misconduct but do not report it because they are afraid of the consequences.
    
- **Reasons:** They worry about damaging their own careers, incurring the anger of the offender, or simply feeling that their report might be ignored.
    

### 3. Impact of Peer Influence

- **Problem:** Employees often fall into the trap of thinking, "If everyone is doing it, it must be right".
    
- **Mechanism:** People are more likely to lapse into bad behavior if they see their co-workers doing it without getting caught. Research shows people are more likely to emulate questionable behaviors exhibited by people they perceive as similar to themselves.
    

### 4. Going Down a Slippery Slope

- **Problem:** Misconduct often starts with small offenses, such as exaggerating a mileage report.
    
- **Progression:** If these small acts go unchecked, they grow into larger offenses like falsifying expenses or outright misuse .
    
- **Habit:** As opportunities to behave unethically grow, people rationalize their misconduct until it becomes a habit.
    

### 5. Setting a Bad Example

- **Problem:** Ethical behavior starts at the top. If leaders do not demonstrate personal character, it sets a weak tone for the entire company .
    
- **Mechanism:** Employees emulate their leaders. For example, if employees see the boss leaving work early every day, they are likely to do the same.



# Ethical decision making process

### 1. The 7-Step Framework for Resolving Ethical Problems

This is the standard procedure to follow when facing an issue.

1. **Gather relevant facts and identify the problem:**
    
    - Ask if you have all the facts or if you are making assumptions.
        
    - Determine if it is really your problem to solve.
        
2. **Identify the affected parties:**
    
    - Identify individuals, organizations, and key stakeholders.
        
    - Determine how they are affected and if there are conflicts between them.
        
3. **Consider the ethical issues involved:**
    
    - Refer to the relevant Code of Ethics.
        
    - Consider if the issue affects the reputation of the profession or public interest.
        
4. **Identify which fundamental principles are affected:**
    
    - Check for threats to principles like **Integrity**, **Objectivity**, **Confidentiality**, and **Professional Behavior** .
        
    - Assess threats like self-interest, familiarity, or intimidation .
        
5. **Refer to internal procedures:**
    
    - Check your organization's policies and procedures for guidance.
        
    - Look for whistleblowing procedures or escalation paths.
        
6. **Evaluate alternative courses of action:**
    
    - Consider applicable laws, regulations, and universal values.
        
    - **Test your decision:** Ask if the action would stand the test of time or scrutiny from peers and family.
        
7. **Implement and monitor:**
    
    - Document your thought process and decisions.
        
    - Implement the action and monitor its progress to justify your course of action later if needed.
        

---

### 2. The PLUS Decision-Making Model

To help filter your options during the process, your notes recommend the **PLUS Model**. This gives you four "ethical filters" to apply to any decision .

- **P - Policies:** Is the decision in line with the company's policies?.
    
- **L - Legal:** Will this violate any legal parameters or regulations?.
    
- **U - Universal:** Does it align with the core values and culture of the organization?.
    
- **S - Self:** Does it meet my own standards of fairness and justice?.
    

---

### 3. Strategy for Ethical Dilemmas (Three-Step Approach)

When you are stuck between two conflicting options (a dilemma), use this simplified strategy:

1. **Analyze the Consequences:** Look at the positive and negative outcomes. A "high quality" good (like health) is better than a "low quality" good (like a new phone).
    
2. **Analyze the Actions:** Determine if the action itself is inherently good (truth-telling) or bad (coercion), regardless of the result.
    
3. **Make a Decision:** Combine the analysis of consequences and actions to choose the best path





# Ethical models for decision making 

### 1. The Utilitarian Approach

This approach focuses on **consequences** (Teleology).

- **Principle:** The ethical action is the one that produces the **greatest good and does the least harm** for all stakeholders involved.
    
- **Focus:** It tries to increase the good done and reduce the harm done.
    
- **Stakeholders:** It considers the impact on clients, colleagues, and the community.
    

### 2. The Rights Approach

This approach focuses on **respect for human dignity** (Deontology).

- **Principle:** The ethical action is the one that best protects and respects the **moral rights** of those affected.
    
- **Key Concept:** Humans have the ability to choose what they do with their lives. They have a right to be treated as "ends" in themselves and not merely as "puppets" or means to an end.
    
- **Examples of Rights:** The right to be told the truth, the right not to be injured, and the right to privacy.
    

### 3. The Fairness/Justice Approach

This approach focuses on **equality and impartiality**.

- **Principle:** Ethical actions should treat all human beings equally. If they are treated unequally, it must be based on some standard that is defensible.
    
- **Example:** It is considered fair to pay people more based on their greater contribution to the organization. Unjust discrimination is ethically wrong.
    

### 4. The Common Good Approach

This approach focuses on **community and society**.

- **Principle:** Interactions with your community are the basis of ethical reasoning.
    
- **Focus:** It requires respect and compassion for all others, especially the vulnerable.
    
- **Application:** It draws attention to marginalized groups, such as Persons with Disabilities (PwD).
    

### 5. The Virtue Approach

This approach focuses on **character and habits**.

- **Principle:** Virtues are dispositions and habits that enable us to act according to the highest potential of our character.
    
- **Key Question:** Instead of asking "What are the rules?", this approach asks, **"What kind of person will I become if I do this?"** or "Is this action consistent with acting at my best?".
    
- **Examples:** Honesty, courage, compassion, integrity, fairness, and self-control.




# Limitations of kohlberg's model

### 1. Designed for General Context, Not Business

- **The Limitation:** The study material explicitly notes that Kohlberg's model was **"not specifically designed for business contexts"**.
    
- **Explanation:** The theory was developed based on psychology and the study of children and general social dilemmas (like the Heinz dilemma). In a business environment, decision-making involves complex variables like profit motives, corporate culture, and shareholder pressure, which the original model does not fully account for.
    

### 2. Reasoning vs. Behavior

- **The Limitation:** The theory focuses on **moral reasoning** (how you think), not necessarily **moral behavior** (what you actually do).
    
- **Explanation:** The notes highlight that Kohlberg was interested in the "reasoning behind their judgments" rather than the decision itself.
    
- **Implication:** A person might be at a high stage of reasoning (Stage 5 or 6) intellectually, but under pressure from a boss or due to fear of job loss, they might act at a lower stage (Stage 1 or 2). Knowing _what_ is right doesn't guarantee _doing_ what is right.
    
	
### 3. Cultural and Individual Differences

- **The Limitation:** The model assumes a progression that is universal, but the notes suggest that "moral philosophies are often used to defend a particular type of economic system" and that values are influenced by culture and upbringing.
    
- **Explanation:** Kohlberg’s stages (especially the highest ones focusing on individual rights and universal abstract principles) are often criticized for being biased toward Western, individualistic cultures. In many Eastern or collectivist cultures (like the Indian traditions discussed in Unit 6), "duty" and "social harmony" (Stage 3/4) might be viewed as higher virtues than individual rights (Stage 5).
    

### 4. Rigid Staging

- **The Limitation:** The theory implies people are "in" a specific stage.
    
- **Reality:** The notes clarify that moral philosophies should be assessed on a **"continuum rather than as static entities"**.
    
- **Explanation:** Most individuals "shift between different moral philosophies" depending on the situation. A manager might use Stage 4 reasoning (Law & Order) for a tax issue but Stage 2 reasoning (Self-interest) for a salary negotiation.
